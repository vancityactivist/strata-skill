# Act Review Checklists

Structured checklists for the most common "is this compliant?" reviews. Use these alongside `strata-property-act.md` — the checklist gives the questions; the act reference gives the thresholds and section numbers.

## Reviewing a general meeting notice

1. Was notice given to every person entitled to receive it under s. 45 (owners, and tenants/mortgagees who have given notice of their entitlement)?
2. Does the notice period meet the minimum — 14 days, plus 4 days deemed delivery if mailed (s. 61(3))?
3. Does the notice include the agenda, the full text of any resolution requiring a 3/4 or unanimous vote, proxy information, and (for the AGM) the proposed budget and financial statements?
4. Is the resolution wording precise enough that owners know exactly what they are approving (amounts, funding source, scope of works)?
5. Consequence check: a defective notice can render resolutions voidable at the CRT — note the cure (re-notice, or ratification at a properly called meeting).

## Reviewing a proposed expenditure

1. Which fund pays — operating fund, contingency reserve fund (CRF), or special levy?
2. What approval threshold applies? Operating: in the approved budget. CRF: 3/4 vote at a general meeting, or majority vote if the expenditure is recommended in the most recent depreciation report (s. 96). Special levy: 3/4 vote (s. 108).
3. Was that approval actually obtained, with valid notice and quorum?
4. If unapproved: does it fit s. 98 — within the bylaw spending limit (or the default in s. 98(2) if no bylaw), or a genuine emergency to ensure safety or prevent significant loss or damage? "Urgent" is not automatically "emergency."
5. Conflict check (s. 32): does any council member or the agent have an interest in the contractor? If so, was it disclosed and did the member abstain?

## Reviewing bylaw/by-law enforceability

1. Was the bylaw validly adopted — 3/4 vote at a general meeting with the full text in the notice (s. 128)?
2. Was the amendment filed at the Land Title Office? Bylaw amendments are not enforceable until filed (s. 128(3)).
3. Does it contravene the SPA, the Regulation, the Human Rights Code, or any other enactment (s. 121)? Watch the statutory limits specifically: rental restriction bylaws are largely invalid since the 2022 amendments, and age restrictions are limited to 55+ (s. 123.1).
4. Is it significantly unfair in operation against particular owners (CRT/court remedy)?

## Reviewing a fine / enforcement action

1. Did the corporation give the owner/tenant written particulars of the complaint (s. 135(1)(e))?
2. Was a reasonable opportunity to respond — and a hearing, if requested — given **before** the fine was imposed? Fines imposed without completing the s. 135 process are invalid; the CRT enforces this strictly.
3. Was the s. 135 decision given to the person in writing as soon as feasible (s. 135(2))?
4. Is the fine within the limits in the bylaws and the Regulation (commonly $200 per contravention; $500 for short-term-rental bylaws; check current Reg. 7.1)?
5. Is the underlying bylaw actually enforceable (see bylaw checklist above)?
6. Is enforcement consistent with how the corporation has treated other owners? Selective enforcement supports a significant-unfairness claim.
7. For continuing contraventions: does s. 135(3) apply (no new notice needed for a continuing contravention once the process was followed), and does the repeat-fine interval comply with Reg. 7.1(2)?

## Repairs responsibility triage

1. Locate the boundary: is the failed component common property, limited common property, or part of the strata lot? Check the registered strata plan and the SPA s. 1 / s. 68 definitions, not assumptions (pipes and wiring servicing more than one lot are common property; the lot boundary is the midpoint of walls/floors/ceilings unless the plan says otherwise).
2. Identify the duty holder: the corporation repairs and maintains common property and common assets (s. 72, Standard Bylaw 8 — including some lot components like the building exterior, windows, and doors on the exterior); owners maintain their lots (Standard Bylaw 2). Check whether the Standard Bylaws have been amended.
3. If the corporation has the duty, it is effectively strict — budget shortfall is not a defence, though the standard is reasonableness in how repairs are carried out.
4. Insurance overlay: is this an insurable event under the corporation's policy (ss. 149–152)? Who bears the deductible — and is there a bylaw or s. 158(2) basis to charge it back to an owner whose act or omission caused the loss?
5. Document the timeline — delay is the main driver of damages claims against corporations at the CRT.
