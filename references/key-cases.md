# Key Cases — Leading BC Strata Authorities

Statute tells the agent what the rule is; these decisions tell council *why it will lose* if it ignores the rule. Citing the leading authority materially strengthens advice and correspondence — a council that shrugs at "s. 135" tends to listen to "the Court of Appeal set aside $7,210 in fines over this."

Use these only where they genuinely apply, and verify the citation on CanLII (canlii.org) before it goes into anything formal. CRT decisions are also published and citable — search the CRT's decision database for on-point facts when a dispute is headed there.

## Bylaw enforcement and fines

**Terry v. The Owners, Strata Plan NW 309, 2016 BCCA 449** — the controlling authority on s. 135. Strict compliance with the s. 135 procedure (written particulars of the complaint, reasonable opportunity to answer, hearing if requested) is mandatory before *any* fine; there is no leeway, and after-the-fact compliance does not cure a fine already imposed. The Court of Appeal set aside $7,210 in accumulated fines. Cite it whenever a council wants to fine first and ask questions later — and note the fines in Terry were for a money debt (short-paid strata fees), an area where fining is especially fraught.

## Significant unfairness

**Dollan v. The Owners, Strata Plan BCS 1589, 2012 BCCA 44** — the test for significantly unfair conduct (now applied through CRT and court remedies): (1) objectively, does the evidence support the owner's asserted reasonable expectation? (2) was that expectation violated by action that is significantly unfair — "burdensome, harsh, wrongful, lacking in probity or fair dealing, or done in bad faith"? Cite it when advising on selective enforcement, denial of alteration requests, or any decision that singles an owner out.

## Repair and maintenance (s. 72)

**Weir v. Owners, Strata Plan NW 17, 2010 BCSC 784** — the corporation's s. 72 duty to repair is fundamental, but the standard is *reasonableness*, not perfection, and courts start from deference to the council's choice among reasonable repair strategies (including "good, better, best" cost trade-offs). Cite it both ways: to push a stalling council to act, and to defend a council that chose a cheaper-but-reasonable repair against an owner demanding the gold-plated option.

## How to use case law in this skill's outputs

- In **advice to council**: name the case, the court, and the consequence in one sentence. The point is credibility, not a legal memo.
- In **letters to owners**: cite the statute, not the case — case citations in correspondence read as escalation. The exception is responding to an owner's lawyer.
- If a matter turns on *applying* one of these tests to contested facts (e.g., whether enforcement was significantly unfair), that's a signal for legal advice, not just a citation.
