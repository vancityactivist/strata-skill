# Privacy (PIPA) and Human Rights Accommodation

Two areas where strata corporations are governed by statutes *outside* the SPA, and where agents most often get caught out. Written from primary sources: BC's Personal Information Protection Act (PIPA) and OIPC guidance; the BC Human Rights Code and tribunal practice.

## Privacy — PIPA applies to strata corporations

A strata corporation is an "organization" under PIPA. That carries real obligations independent of the SPA:

- **Privacy officer**: every strata corporation must designate one (often the council secretary or the strata manager) and have a **written privacy policy**. The privacy officer ensures compliance — they are *not* a gatekeeper who withholds information from council; council still needs the information required to perform its duties.
- **Collection, use, disclosure**: only what a reasonable person would consider appropriate, generally with consent. Owner/tenant lists, banking details, hardship applications, and correspondence are all personal information.
- **Video surveillance and key-fob systems**: before installing, the corporation should pass a bylaw authorizing the system and adopt a written policy covering purpose, retention, and access. OIPC orders accept surveillance for genuine *security* purposes but not for enforcing minor bylaws, especially where less invasive measures exist. Signage disclosing surveillance is expected.
- **Doorbell cameras on common property** raise the same issues at the owner level — alteration approval plus privacy reasonableness.
- **Interaction with SPA records access**: ss. 35–36 give owners the right to many records, and PIPA does not override that — but redact what s. 36 doesn't require (e.g., other owners' banking details). The two regimes must be read together.
- **Complaints** go first to the corporation's privacy officer, then to the Office of the Information and Privacy Commissioner (oipc.bc.ca).

When advising: check the corporation actually has a privacy policy and designated officer. Most don't — flagging that gap is high-value, low-effort best practice.

## Human rights — the duty to accommodate

The BC Human Rights Code prevails over the SPA and over any bylaw (SPA s. 121 makes bylaws contravening the Code unenforceable). Strata corporations provide "accommodation, service or facility customarily available to the public" (Code s. 8) and so owe a **duty to accommodate** protected characteristics (disability, age, family status, etc.) to the point of *undue hardship*.

Common scenarios for agents:

- **Pets as accommodation**: a no-pets bylaw cannot be applied to a person who needs an animal for a disability-related reason. Certified guide/service dogs (Guide Dog and Service Dog Act) have statutory access rights; emotional-support animals can still attract Code protection with medical evidence. Ask for supporting documentation politely and generically — diagnosis details cannot be demanded, only confirmation of a disability-related need.
- **Physical accessibility**: ramp, lift, parking, or door-operator requests engage the duty to accommodate; cost alone is rarely undue hardship for a corporation of any size. Process the request seriously, get quotes, document the analysis.
- **55+ age bylaws** (SPA s. 123.1): exemptions are statutory (s. 123.2) — existing residents are grandfathered and live-in caregivers are exempt — and were *expanded by Order in Council in May 2023*; verify the current exemption list before advising enforcement.
- **Procedure matters**: accommodation requests should go through a documented, individualized process — acknowledge, gather information, consider options, decide with reasons. A corporation that ignores or summarily denies a request loses at the Human Rights Tribunal on process alone.

**Escalation rule**: any accommodation dispute that survives a first, good-faith attempt to resolve it should go to a lawyer — Human Rights Tribunal complaints carry uncapped damages for injury to dignity, and the corporation (not the agent) wears the award. Say this specifically when it applies.
