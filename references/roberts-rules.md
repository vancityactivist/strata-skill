# Robert's Rules of Order in BC Strata Meetings

Parliamentary procedure for chairing and advising strata general meetings and council meetings. Robert's Rules (RONR) is the convention most BC stratas reach for, but it is a *servant* of the meeting, not law — get the hierarchy right before getting procedural.

## The hierarchy — what governs when

1. **Strata Property Act and Regulation** — always win. Voting thresholds (majority / 3/4 / unanimous), quorum, notice, proxies, and who may chair are statutory; no procedural ruling can change them.
2. **The strata corporation's bylaws** — many bylaws expressly adopt Robert's Rules for general meetings where the Act and bylaws are silent; others are silent entirely. Check the registered bylaws first.
3. **Robert's Rules** — fills the gaps: how motions are made, debated, amended, and disposed of. If the bylaws don't adopt them, the chair may still announce them as the meeting's procedure; consistency and fairness matter more than the rulebook chosen.

When advising, never present a RONR rule as binding if it conflicts with the SPA — e.g., RONR's two-thirds vote to close debate does not convert any SPA voting threshold, and RONR quorum concepts are displaced by s. 48.

## Chairing a general meeting — the working sequence

1. **Before the chair takes over**: registration, proxy certification, quorum count (1/3 of votes in person or by proxy, s. 48). No quorum → follow the bylaws/s. 48(2)-(3) adjournment rules.
2. **Who chairs**: the council president, or as the bylaws provide; the meeting may elect a chair if needed. A professional chair (often the strata manager, if the bylaws and the room allow) is best practice for contested meetings — but the chair must be, and appear, neutral.
3. **Order of business** follows the agenda in the notice. New substantive business not in the notice cannot be *decided* if it required notice (any 3/4 or unanimous resolution); it can only be discussed.
4. **Each motion**: moved → seconded → stated by the chair → debated → put to a vote → result announced with the count where a precise threshold matters. For 3/4 votes, announce the exact for/against count and have it minuted — "carried" alone invites challenge.

## Motions the chair must know

| Motion | Needs second? | Debatable? | Vote (RONR default) | Strata notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Main motion | Yes | Yes | Majority | Threshold comes from the SPA, not RONR, for statutory matters |
| Amend | Yes | Yes | Majority | See the amendment trap below |
| Refer to committee | Yes | Yes | Majority | Useful to defuse a hostile room |
| Postpone to a set time | Yes | Yes | Majority | Cannot postpone past statutory deadlines (e.g., budget approval) |
| Previous question (close debate) | Yes | No | 2/3 | Procedural only — does not change the main motion's SPA threshold |
| Table | Yes | No | Majority | In practice often misused to kill motions; prefer postpone |
| Point of order | No | No | Chair rules | Raised immediately, not after the vote |
| Appeal the chair's ruling | Yes | Limited | Majority | The room, not the chair, is the final procedural authority |
| Adjourn | Yes | No | Majority | Check bylaws for reconvening requirements |

## The amendment trap (the most common strata-specific error)

RONR allows free amendment of main motions. The SPA does not, for noticed 3/4-vote resolutions: **s. 50(2) permits amending the proposed wording at the meeting only if the amendment (a) does not substantially change the resolution and (b) is itself approved by a 3/4 vote, taken before the vote on the resolution.** Both conditions are statutory. A change that alters the substance (amount up, different purpose, different funding source) fails condition (a) no matter how the room votes — and owners who gave proxies on the original text are the ones with standing to complain. When the room wants a materially different resolution: defeat or withdraw the original, and re-notice the new version for an SGM. Advise chairs to say exactly that from the chair.

## Council meetings

Council meetings are less formal — RONR "small board rules" fit: the chair may participate and vote (subject to the bylaws), motions need no second, and discussion may precede a formal motion. What still matters: quorum of council per the bylaws, s. 32 conflict-of-interest recusals, owners' right to attend as observers (if the bylaws so provide, as Standard Bylaw 17 does), and minutes that record *decisions* and vote outcomes, not debate.

## Minute-taking and procedure

Minutes record what was *done*, not what was *said*: motion text as finally voted, mover (seconder optional in minutes), the result with counts for elevated thresholds, and any procedural rulings or recusals. Procedural skirmishes (points of order, appeals) are minuted as ruling and outcome in one line.

## Chair's script fragments

- Stating a motion: "It is moved and seconded that [text]. Is there any discussion?"
- Putting the question: "All those in favour of the resolution as circulated, please raise your voting cards... Opposed?... The resolution passes with [X] in favour, [Y] opposed, achieving the required 3/4 majority."
- Out-of-scope amendment: "That amendment would materially change the resolution that was circulated with the notice. Owners who gave proxies did so on the original text, so I can't accept it. The options are to vote on the resolution as circulated, or defeat it and have council bring the revised version to a special general meeting."
- Restoring order: "I'll take speakers in turn — two minutes each, and please address the chair."
